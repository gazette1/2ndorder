# NAGE: Reshoring beneficiary thesis lacks evidence; company sells Niagen for compounding and dietary supplements, not domestic API manufacturing.

## Bear case first

The thesis assumes Niagen Bioscience is a domestic API producer benefiting from reshoring. The filings show the company sells Niagen as a dietary ingredient and for compounding by FDA-registered 503B facilities. No filing mentions API manufacturing, tariffs as an opportunity, or reshoring. Customer evidence is thin: no named enterprise customers, no federal awards. Insider buying is minimal at $45,616. The company has a shelf registration, indicating potential dilution. The composite score of 18/100 reflects low optionality, no catalysts, and no management conviction on reshoring.

## Business today

Niagen Bioscience sells nicotinamide riboside (Niagen) in food-grade form for dietary supplements and in pharmaceutical-grade form for compounding by FDA-registered 503B outsourcing facilities [0][1][2]. Revenue was $129,423,000 and cash $44,500,000 (SEC XBRL). The company's products are used by compounding pharmacies and clinics [8].

## Chain position

The company is a supplier of a single ingredient to compounding pharmacies and supplement manufacturers. It does not manufacture finished pharmaceutical products or APIs for generic or branded drugs. The economic logic of reshoring applies to API manufacturing, but Niagen is not an API; it is a dietary ingredient with FDA GRAS and NDI status [9][10][11]. The chain position is a specialty ingredient supplier to a niche compounding market.

## Customer graph

No named enterprise customers are disclosed. No federal awards (USASpending). Six other filers name the company in their filings, but the identities are not specified. Customer concentration risk cannot be assessed. The customer base appears to be compounding pharmacies and clinics, which are unproven as a concentrated source of revenue.

## Insider and management signal

Management language is focused on regulatory compliance [14][15] and does not mention reshoring, capacity expansion, or quantified targets. No statements in filings indicate conviction in a reshoring theme.

## Street view

No sell-side coverage is identified. Consensus and mean target are not available. The lack of analyst attention is consistent with a small-cap company with low institutional interest. No recent firm rating actions are reported.

## Non-linear case

For a multiple to occur, the company would need to transition from a dietary ingredient supplier to a domestic manufacturer of pharmaceutical-grade Niagen for large-scale prescription use, driven by policy mandates. This would require FDA approval of Niagen as a drug, not just compounding authorization. The filings show no investment in manufacturing capacity or drug development. The company's cash of $44.5M is modest for such a pivot. Without evidence of a strategic shift, the non-linear case is speculative.

- The company is subject to various U.S. federal regulations [12][13][14][15].

## Sizing and survivability

Average daily dollar volume is $3.3M, requiring about 11 trading days to build a position. Net cash is $39.5M (reality check). Share count increased 2.4% in 12 months, and a shelf registration is on file, indicating potential dilution. The company has sufficient cash but limited growth visibility. A small fund could own it, but liquidity is moderate.

## What would change our mind

- A filing (8-K or 10-K) announcing a new U.S. manufacturing facility or long-term supply agreement for pharmaceutical-grade Niagen.
- Form 4 showing significant insider buying (e.g., over $1M) or a CEO purchase.
- A federal award (USASpending) designating Niagen as a strategic ingredient.
