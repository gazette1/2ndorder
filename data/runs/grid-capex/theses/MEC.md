# MEC: Rising electricity costs from grid capex supercycle erode margins in energy-intensive metal treating services with limited pricing power

## Bear case first
MEC’s broad finishing capabilities [1] may allow it to differentiate and retain customers, mitigating the need to fully absorb electricity cost increases. Proceeds from the recent offering could be deployed into energy-efficient equipment [0], offsetting margin pressure. The absence of insider selling (Form 4) suggests management sees no near-term distress. However, no evidence confirms pricing power or efficiency investments; the bull case rests on unproven assumptions.

## Business today
MEC provides metal fabrication, heat treating, and finishing services (shot blasting, e-coating, powder coating, CARC) to OEMs in agriculture, construction, power generation, and military. Revenue was $546.5MM, cash $1.5MM as of 2025-12-31 (XBRL). Capital expenditures are capped under credit agreement covenants [8].

## Chain position
MEC occupies an at-risk node in metal treating and finishing (order 2). Rising electricity costs directly increase operating expenses for energy-intensive ovens, furnaces, and plating lines, while a fragmented industry limits pass-through to customers.

## Customer graph
No named enterprise customers in filings; no federal awards (USASpending). Four other filers name MEC (customer graph). Customer concentration is unproven; reliance on a few large OEMs is possible but not disclosed.

## Insider and management signal
No open-market insider buys or sells over the trailing 12 months (Form 4). Management language in filings is generic; no specific commitments to grid or data center opportunities [0][2][3]. 2026 capex guidance of $15M–$20M [10] is modest and unchanged.

## Street view
No known sell-side coverage; consensus n/a, mean target n/a. Thin coverage is typical for a small-cap with $315MM float; the stock is under-researched and potentially mispriced.

## Non-linear case
Downside scenario: grid capex drives structural electricity rate increases of 20–30% over 2026–2030. MEC cannot pass through costs; operating margins compress from mid-single digits to near zero. Free cash flow turns negative; the company breaches credit agreement leverage covenants, triggering restricted capex or forced asset sales. Smaller competitors close, but MEC’s own cash exhaustion leads to equity dilution or distressed sale. The path requires sustained rate hikes and no mitigating investments.

## Evidence
- MEC intends to use proceeds for reducing debt, growth capex, and working capital [0].
- MEC has broad finishing capabilities including shot blasting, e-coating, powder coating, CARC [1].
- Cash flow dependent on operating and financing sources; future capex may be reduced or delayed [2].
- Management assumptions for income approach include sales, margins, operating costs, etc. [3].
- Free cash flow declined from $77.7M (2024) to $26.9M (2025) [4], primarily due to lower operating cash flow [5].
- Increase in cash used in investing due to Accu-Fab acquisition [7].
- Credit agreement restricts annual capital expenditures [8].
- 2026 capex expected $15M–$20M [10].
- Company monitors additional capital sources [11]; future cash flows subject to variables [12].
- No assurance operations provide sufficient cash [13].
- Acquisitions may require reducing capex or seeking capital [14].
- Without funds, may not complete acquisitions or finance necessary capex [15].

## What would change our mind
1. Filing (10-Q or 8-K) showing MEC signing long-term contracts with pricing pass-through clauses for energy surcharges.
2. Form 4 showing open-market insider buying indicating confidence in margin recovery.
3. Federal award (USASpending) for data center-related finishing work.
