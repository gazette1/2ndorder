# WULF: Data center developer exposed to rising interconnection costs from the US grid capex supercycle

## Bear case first
The thesis relies on a bottleneck that raises TeraWulf’s own development costs. Insider selling of $31.1MM net over 10 buys and 15 sales (Form 4) signals conviction is weak inside the firm. No named customer has been disclosed, and zero federal awards appear in USASpending. Without a contract that ties the 1+ GW campus to a paying tenant, the acquisition merely adds land and power rights that may never generate revenue.

## Business today
TeraWulf develops and operates large-scale digital infrastructure campuses for AI and high-performance computing. Revenue was $13.4MM and cash was $3266.4MM as of 2020-12-31 (SEC XBRL). The business today is a small fraction of the 1+ GW opportunity described.

## Chain position
TeraWulf occupies the data center developer node (order 2, at_risk). The economic logic is that developers compete with grid operators for the same transformers and switchgear; as utilities impose larger interconnection cost allocations and lead times extend, project returns fall. The supercycle they help create thus works against their own economics.

## Customer graph
No named enterprise customers exist in the filings. Federal awards are $0 (USASpending). Zero other filers name the company in their disclosures. Customer evidence is thin; the revenue base remains unproven beyond the existing small figure.

## Insider and management signal
Insiders have sold a net $31.1MM in open-market transactions over the trailing 12 months (Form 4). Management language is highly specific: “defining constraint is power” and the site “Muskie” is described with 1+ GW capacity, a 345 kV substation, and a 765 kV network [4][5]. The verbal conviction is strong, but insider actions contradict it.

## Street view
Sell-side coverage is absent: no analysts, no consensus, no mean target. For a company with $13MM revenue and a $1.3BN market cap, the lack of analyst research is itself a sign that the market has not yet priced the potential.

## Non-linear case
If TeraWulf completes the 345 kV substation, secures a named hyperscaler tenant, and builds out the full 1+ GW campus, revenue could multiply by a factor of 100 or more. The defining constraint is power, and the Muskie site has redundant 765 kV transmission and already-zoned land [1][3]. The outcome requires execution certainty and a customer commitment, neither of which is demonstrated today.

## Evidence
- The acquisition expands the portfolio of large-scale digital infrastructure campuses and advances the strategy of developing shovel-ready AI/HPC sites. [0]
- Kentucky Power is constructing a 345 kV substation connected to the 765 kV network, designed to support the full 1+ GW campus. [1]
- Transmission infrastructure and energy service agreements were executed concurrently with the acquisition under the applicable Industrial General Service tariff. [2]
- The site is zoned, permitting is underway, and limited site work is required. [3]
- Management stated: “the defining constraint in this market is no longer computing hardware, it is power, transmission infrastructure, and execution certainty.” [4]
- Management described Muskie as combining scalable power, robust transmission, development readiness, and strategic positioning that is difficult to replicate. [5]

## What would change our mind
- A named customer contract or letter of intent filed on an 8-K or in a material definitive agreement.
- A Form 4 showing an insider open-market purchase of at least $1MM in a single transaction.
- A federal award (USASpending) or a utility interconnection milestone filing that confirms the substation is complete and energized.
