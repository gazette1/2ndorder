Theses

NWE: Regulated utility playing the grid buildout – but without the data center direct exposure

Bear case first

NorthWestern’s $3.2B capital plan is funded by debt and equity, not by new data-center-driven load. The company has no named customer or program tied to hyperscale demand. Its 4–6% growth target is in line with baseline utility growth, not a supercycle. Insider buying is $0 – zero conviction from those closest to the business. The $209M in federal awards is for health services, not grid infrastructure, and no other filings name a data center customer. The thesis that grid capex for data centers will boost NWE assumes a channel that the evidence does not support.

Business today

NorthWestern is a regulated electric and gas utility operating primarily in Montana and South Dakota. For 2025, it reported revenue of $414.3MM and cash of $8.8MM (SEC XBRL). Earnings are driven by rate base; the company targets 4–6% annual rate base and EPS growth [0][2].

Chain position

NWE occupies the *regulated transmission and distribution* node of the grid capex chain. Its economic logic is that new generation interconnection (wind, solar) and regional transmission projects (e.g., North Plains Connector) require utility-owned infrastructure. However, the company does not explicitly reference data center load or interconnection queue bottlenecks – the seed thesis mechanism.

Customer graph

No named enterprise or data center customer. Federal awards total $209MM from the Department of Health and Human Services (USASpending) – not grid related. Two other filers name NWE in SEC filings (customer graph). Customer evidence is thin; the revenue base is regulated tariffs, not contracted commercial offtake.

Insider and management signal

Insider open-market activity: $0 net, 0 buys, 0 sales by 0 insiders over trailing 12 months (Form 4). Management language is quantified and confident: 4–6% growth targets, $3.2B capital plan, equity issuance beginning 2027 [2][3]. Absence of insider buying is a neutral-to-negative read given the stated conviction.

Street view

No sell-side consensus, mean target, or analyst count is available from the provided evidence. Coverage is effectively zero. For a $3.1B float company, this is thin and leaves room for a catalyst-driven re-rate – or for the thesis to remain unrecognized.

Non-linear case

For NWE to return a multiple of today’s business, the grid capex supercycle must translate into explicit data-center-related transmission projects that bypass normal rate case lags. The $700M North Plains Connector grant [6] could be the first domino – if it attracts co-located data center load that NWE serves. The company would need to file a major new transmission project with named customer(s) and accelerate equity issuance to capture the upside before rate base growth.

WULF: Data center developer exposed to rising interconnection costs from the US grid capex supercycle

Bear case first

The thesis relies on a bottleneck that raises TeraWulf’s own development costs. Insider selling of $31.1MM net over 10 buys and 15 sales (Form 4) signals conviction is weak inside the firm. No named customer has been disclosed, and zero federal awards appear in USASpending. Without a contract that ties the 1+ GW campus to a paying tenant, the acquisition merely adds land and power rights that may never generate revenue.

Business today

TeraWulf develops and operates large-scale digital infrastructure campuses for AI and high-performance computing. Revenue was $13.4MM and cash was $3266.4MM as of 2020-12-31 (SEC XBRL). The business today is a small fraction of the 1+ GW opportunity described.

Chain position

TeraWulf occupies the data center developer node (order 2, at_risk). The economic logic is that developers compete with grid operators for the same transformers and switchgear; as utilities impose larger interconnection cost allocations and lead times extend, project returns fall. The supercycle they help create thus works against their own economics.

Customer graph

No named enterprise customers exist in the filings. Federal awards are $0 (USASpending). Zero other filers name the company in their disclosures. Customer evidence is thin; the revenue base remains unproven beyond the existing small figure.

Insider and management signal

Insiders have sold a net $31.1MM in open-market transactions over the trailing 12 months (Form 4). Management language is highly specific: “defining constraint is power” and the site “Muskie” is described with 1+ GW capacity, a 345 kV substation, and a 765 kV network [4][5]. The verbal conviction is strong, but insider actions contradict it.

Street view

Sell-side coverage is absent: no analysts, no consensus, no mean target. For a company with $13MM revenue and a $1.3BN market cap, the lack of analyst research is itself a sign that the market has not yet priced the potential.

Non-linear case

If TeraWulf completes the 345 kV substation, secures a named hyperscaler tenant, and builds out the full 1+ GW campus, revenue could multiply by a factor of 100 or more. The defining constraint is power, and the Muskie site has redundant 765 kV transmission and already-zoned land [1][3]. The outcome requires execution certainty and a customer commitment, neither of which is demonstrated today.

Evidence

  • The acquisition expands the portfolio of large-scale digital infrastructure campuses and advances the strategy of developing shovel-ready AI/HPC sites. [0]
  • Kentucky Power is constructing a 345 kV substation connected to the 765 kV network, designed to support the full 1+ GW campus. [1]
  • Transmission infrastructure and energy service agreements were executed concurrently with the acquisition under the applicable Industrial General Service tariff. [2]
  • The site is zoned, permitting is underway, and limited site work is required. [3]
  • Management stated: “the defining constraint in this market is no longer computing hardware, it is power, transmission infrastructure, and execution certainty.” [4]
  • Management described Muskie as combining scalable power, robust transmission, development readiness, and strategic positioning that is difficult to replicate. [5]

What would change our mind

  • A named customer contract or letter of intent filed on an 8-K or in a material definitive agreement.
  • A Form 4 showing an insider open-market purchase of at least $1MM in a single transaction.
  • A federal award (USASpending) or a utility interconnection milestone filing that confirms the substation is complete and energized.

RIOT: Data center pivot is speculative; core mining revenue faces headwinds from grid congestion and absent customer validation

Bear case first

The company’s pivot to data centers is aspirational, with no named enterprise customers and zero federal awards (USASpending). Management’s forward-looking statements [0] lack quantified revenue targets, and the Rockdale lease NOI excludes capex and other material costs [1]. Insider trading is absent over the trailing 12 months (Form 4), signaling no conviction from those closest. The $647MM mining revenue (XBRL) is at risk from Bitcoin price volatility and rising competition, while the data center opportunity remains unquantified. Without customer validation, the thesis that grid congestion will yield cheap power for data centers is unsubstantiated.

Business today

Riot Platforms mines Bitcoin, earning revenue from mining rewards and power curtailment credits from ERCOT demand response programs [2][3]. As of 2025-12-31, revenue was $647.4MM and cash $233.5MM (XBRL).

Chain position

Riot is a commodity power consumer that buys low-cost electricity for energy-intensive computing. The seed thesis posits that grid congestion will increase curtailment, lowering power prices in congested regions – a potential benefit if Riot can flexibly curtail mining or repurpose capacity for data centers. The company’s ability to capture that value depends on interconnection agreements and operational flexibility, neither of which is proven.

Customer graph

Riot has no named enterprise customers for its data center plans, no federal awards (USASpending), and zero other filers list the company as a customer. The only revenue source is Bitcoin mining, an anonymous commodity market, not a customer relationship. Customer validation is entirely unproven.

Insider and management signal

No insider bought or sold shares open-market in the trailing 12 months (Form 4). Management provides forward-looking statements on data center development and lease contracts [0] but offers no concrete milestones or quantified targets. The absence of insider transactions suggests either a lack of conviction or restricted trading windows, but no positive signal.

Street view

Sell-side coverage is unknown; no analyst firm, consensus, or target is publicly available. Thin coverage leaves the stock underfollowed, which amplifies volatility but provides room for positive surprise if catalysts emerge.

Non-linear case

The non-linear outcome requires Riot to execute the Rockdale data center lease, secure additional customers, and convert cheap power into recurring NOI as described [0]. If grid congestion drives sustained negative power prices, Riot could monetize both mining and demand response. A successful pivot could multiply revenue from a single-digit multiple to double-digit multiple, but this depends on customer contracts, energization timelines (projected but not dated), and operational execution – all unproven.

Evidence

  • Power curtailment credits from ERCOT demand response programs are recognized outside cost of revenue [2][3].
  • Lease NOI is presented excluding capital expenditures, material financial obligations, and other costs that could materially affect results [1].

  • Insider open-market purchases of at least $1MM aggregate within a quarter (Form 4).
  • A federal award or grant for data center operations recorded in USASpending.

WS: Beneficiary of grid capex supercycle with limited direct evidence of exposure

The filing evidence ties 80% of Worthington’s steel sold to automotive applications (clutch plates and electrical steel laminations) [0]. The company’s $3.1B revenue base makes electrical steel likely a small fraction today. Zero insider trading (Form 4) and no federal awards (USASpending) offer no corroborating signal.

Business today

Worthington Steel sells flat-rolled steel products, with electrical steel laminations and clutch plates representing 80% of steel sold to the North American automotive market [0]. As of May 2025, revenue was $3,093.3MM and cash $38.0MM (SEC XBRL).

Chain position

The company is a producer of electrical steel, a material essential for transformer cores. In a grid capex supercycle, transformer demand rises, tightening electrical steel supply. Volume and price leverage accrue to producers, but Worthington’s current filings show no explicit link to grid or transformer customers.

Customer graph

No named enterprise customers. Federal awards total $0 (USASpending). One other filer names the company. Customer evidence is extremely thin; the primary end market identified is automotive [0].

Insider and management signal

Management language highlights expanding electrical steel capabilities via acquisitions (Sitem in Germany, licensed ablation technology) but provides no quantified targets, capacity timelines, or customer program names [1][2].

Street view

Worthington’s expansion in Canada, Mexico, and Germany [1][2] could capture a meaningful share. With high operating leverage, margins expand non-linearly. Electrical steel, a small portion today, could become the dominant earnings driver, multiplying the current business multiple.

ENS: EnerSys lacks direct exposure to the US grid capex supercycle from data center load

Bear case first

The company’s energy storage offerings address standard applications, demand charge reduction, utility backup, EV charging, with no disclosed investment or contracts tied to grid-scale infrastructure for data centers. Insider net selling of approximately $1M over the trailing 12 months signals low conviction from the one insider who traded (Form 4). Federal awards total $333.4MM, but the two largest are from the Department of Energy ($198.7MM) and Department of Defense ($18.3MM combined), not from investor-owned utilities or data center operators. The filings show no named enterprise customers, no revenue attribution to a grid-capex segment, and no dated catalysts. The seed thesis of a data-center-driven supercycle does not appear in EnerSys’s current evidence.

Business today

EnerSys, through its New Ventures segment, supplies energy storage and management systems for demand charge reduction, utility back-up power, and dynamic fast charging for electric vehicles [0]. As of fiscal Q3 2020, revenue was $216.1MM and cash was $438.7MM (SEC XBRL).

Chain position

EnerSys is a hardware and systems provider in the energy storage supply chain, not a utility or project developer. Its economic logic depends on selling modular storage to commercial and industrial customers, not on capturing utility-rate-base expansion or volumetric rate redesign.

Customer graph

Named enterprise customers: none in the evidence. Government awards total $333.4MM: $198.7MM from the Department of Energy (DEMS0000119), $9.2MM and $9.1MM from the Department of Defense (N0016408CGS07 and 0009) (USASpending). Five other SEC filers name EnerSys as a customer. Customer evidence is thin: a few federal awards and no disclosed commercial accounts.

Insider and management signal

One insider executed 1 buy and 2 sales for net open-market proceeds of approximately -$1M over the trailing 12 months (Form 4). Management language in the sole filing is generic (“various applications”) with no quantified targets, committed capex, or named programs [0].

Street view

Sell-side coverage is unknown; consensus and mean target are not available. The absence of analyst estimates itself indicates the stock is under-covered relative to the grid-capex theme.

Non-linear case

EnerSys could win a large, multi-year grid-scale storage contract from a major utility or a hyperscaler seeking behind-the-meter backup for data centers. Such an award would appear in an 8-K or a new federal award on USASpending. If the company also demonstrated insider buying (Form 4) and management guided to a dedicated grid-capex revenue line, a re-rating would be possible. Today no evidence supports that path.

Evidence

What would change our mind

2. Open-market insider purchases totaling more than $500M (Form 4).

3. A new federal award from the Department of Energy or a utility-specific RFP that exceeds $100MM (USASpending).
